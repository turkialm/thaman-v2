ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper presents THAMAN, an Automated Valuation Model (AVM) for estimating
residential property prices across New York City's five boroughs. The system
integrates structural property attributes with spatial Quality-of-Life (QoL)
indicators — including proximity to transit, crime rates, income demographics,
points-of-interest density, building health signals, and MTA station quality —
into a four-model stacked ensemble comprising two XGBoost models, LightGBM, and
CatBoost, blended by a Ridge meta-learner.

Trained on 185,092 NYC property sales from 2022 to 2026, the final model
(Stack v22) achieves R² = 0.6495 and Median Absolute Percentage Error (MedAPE)
of 20.32% on a time-based holdout set of 27,763 unseen sales across 134 features,
including a novel NTA price trend slope feature capturing neighbourhood-level
price appreciation momentum via per-NTA OLS regression, and ten new building
health and mobility indicators sourced from NYC HPD violation records, DOB permit
filings, granular 311 complaint density (rodent and heat), and MTA station
quality attributes. The system further implements 2026 AVM best practices
including segment-adaptive confidence intervals, a 0–100 confidence score with
letter grade, comparable-count hit-rate signalling, and four quality control
flags (SPARSE_MARKET, LUXURY_SEGMENT, HIGH_UNCERTAINTY, METRO_CORE).

A bilingual (English/Arabic) web application provides interactive map-based
property valuation with SHAP-driven feature explanations including waterfall
visualisations, natural-language tooltips, a segment-adaptive confidence band,
and real-time neighbourhood-level encoding resolved from a 185K-sale spatial
index. The complete system is deployed on Hugging Face Spaces and open-sourced
on GitHub.

The system is extended to Riyadh, Saudi Arabia as a parallel AVM trained on
6,910 district-level real estate transactions (2018–2025) sourced from the Saudi
Open Data Portal. Across 140 features covering metro and bus transit, commercial
density, air quality (NO₂, SO₂, PM₁₀, O₃), QoL proximity to mosques, malls,
schools, hospitals, parks and entertainment venues, macroeconomic indices
(REI quarterly index, Saudi average salary), district price aggregates, and
rental market signals (SA_Aqar), the Riyadh stack achieves OOF R² = 0.9343 /
MedAPE = 8.28% and holdout R² = 0.8003 / MedAPE = 15.56% on a 2025 Q1–Q3
out-of-sample test set of 1,727 district-quarter observations.

Keywords: automated valuation model, ensemble learning, XGBoost, LightGBM,
CatBoost, stacking, target encoding, spatial features, SHAP, New York City,
Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, dual-city AVM, real estate, quality of life indicators,
adaptive confidence, NTA encoding, district-level prediction.

1. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

1.1 Motivation
--------------
Property valuation is a central task in real estate finance, mortgage
underwriting, and urban policy. Traditional appraisal is slow (days to weeks),
expensive ($300–$500 per appraisal), and subject to appraiser bias. Automated
Valuation Models (AVMs) use statistical and machine learning methods to produce
instant price estimates at near-zero marginal cost.

The deployment of AVMs in highly heterogeneous real estate markets presents a
formidable computational challenge at the intersection of data science, urban
economics, and municipal policy. Urban environments characterised by dense
stratification, hyper-local zoning regulations, and vast disparities in
structural typologies require valuation frameworks that transcend simple linear
hedonic pricing or naive comparative market analyses.

Existing commercial AVMs (Zillow Zestimate, Redfin Estimate, CoreLogic) are
proprietary and opaque. Empirical evaluations indicate that while they modestly
outperform naive list prices, they remain highly vulnerable to geographic
heterogeneity and tail risks. Error distributions tend to be heavy-tailed, with
extreme misvaluations concentrated in specific neighbourhoods or among atypical
properties. Furthermore, algorithmic bias presents a systemic risk: analyses
demonstrate that AVMs can systematically yield greater valuation errors for
minority homeowners, producing errors significantly higher in majority-Black
neighbourhoods even after controlling for structural characteristics.

This project addresses four gaps:
  1. Most NYC-focused models use small datasets (5K–50K sales). This work uses
     185K sales across five boroughs and four years.
  2. QoL spatial indicators are rarely integrated end-to-end from raw open data
     into a production-ready inference pipeline.
  3. AVM confidence communication is often binary. This work implements
     segment-adaptive confidence with a formal QC block aligned with 2026 AVM
     industry standards.
  4. Most models ignore neighbourhood price momentum. This work introduces an
     NTA-level log-linear price trend slope as an explicit feature.

1.2 Research Questions
----------------------
  RQ1: Can a stacked ensemble of gradient boosted trees outperform a single
       XGBoost baseline on NYC residential property price prediction?

  RQ2: Do neighbourhood-level spatial QoL features (NTA encoding, transit
       proximity, crime rate, income) improve prediction accuracy over structural
       attributes alone?

  RQ3: How should confidence intervals be communicated to reflect the
       heterogeneous predictability across boroughs and price tiers?

  RQ4: Does incorporating neighbourhood price appreciation momentum (NTA price
       trend slope) further improve model accuracy?

  RQ5: Can the same stacking architecture generalise to a data-scarce emerging
       market (Riyadh) where observations are district-level aggregates rather
       than individual transactions, and how does the OOF-to-holdout gap behave
       when the holdout represents entirely unseen calendar quarters?

1.3 Contributions
-----------------
  - A full end-to-end AVM pipeline from raw NYC open data to deployed web app.
  - A 104-feature dataset combining structural, spatial, temporal, neighbourhood
    momentum, building health, and transit quality signals.
  - Stack v22: a 4-model ensemble achieving R² = 0.6495 / MedAPE = 20.32% on
    27,763 holdout sales, with 10 new v11 features (HPD violations, DOB permits,

  - Segment-adaptive confidence intervals per borough × price tier.

    Includes 133 Riyadh district polygons from OSM and a 12-layer choropleth.

2. RELATED WORK
================================================================================

2.1 The Evolution and Limitations of Traditional AVMs
------------------------------------------------------
Automated valuation algorithms are mathematically based software programs that
market analysts, lenders, and institutions use to produce market value estimates
based on location, market conditions, and real estate characteristics. From their
inception, they have been utilised by government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs)
and financial institutions for underwriting lower loan-to-value mortgages,
facilitating home equity lines of credit (HELOCs), and conducting portfolio-wide
collateral risk assessments.

The baseline performance of contemporary commercial AVMs in dense urban cores
typically yields a MedAPE between 17.5% and 19.8%, with models frequently
overstating administrative assessments by 16–18%. These models routinely
struggle to maintain predictive parity across diverse property classes, often
generating wider error distributions in heterogeneous zip codes or historically
marginalised neighbourhoods — embedding systematic upward biases and heavy-tailed
error distributions that carry severe implications for lending equity and market
transparency.

2.2 Hedonic Price Models
------------------------
The hedonic pricing framework (Rosen, 1974) decomposes property price into
implicit prices of constituent attributes. Early models used OLS regression on
structural features: floor area, number of rooms, age, and location dummies.
These typically achieve R² of 0.5–0.65 on urban datasets (Sirmans et al., 2005).

2.3 Machine Learning for Property Valuation
-------------------------------------------
Gradient boosted trees have become the dominant method in AVM literature.
XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016), LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017), and CatBoost
(Prokhorenkova et al., 2018) consistently outperform OLS and neural networks
on tabular real estate data (Phan, 2018; Truong et al., 2020).

Ensemble stacking — training a meta-learner on out-of-fold predictions of
diverse base models — further improves accuracy by reducing correlated errors
(Wolpert, 1992; Breiman, 1996).

2.4 Spatial and QoL Features
-----------------------------
Proximity to transit is a well-documented price determinant. A New York MTA
study found subway station proximity adds $0–$8K per 100m reduction in walking
distance, with express stations commanding a premium over local stations (Hess
& Almeida, 2007). Park proximity (Lutzenhiser & Netusil, 2001), school quality
(Black, 1999), and crime rates (Ihlanfeldt & Mayock, 2010) have all been shown
to capitalise into residential prices.

2.5 AVM Confidence and Quality
-------------------------------
The 2026 AVM model risk management guidelines recommend that every AVM output
include: (a) a confidence score, (b) a comparable count (hit rate), and
(c) explicit quality flags for data-sparse or atypical segments.

3.2 PLUTO (Primary Land Use Tax Lot Output)
-------------------------------------------
Source:   NYC Department of City Planning
Version:  PLUTO 25v4

PLUTO is an exhaustive compilation of land use, geographic, and building-level
data maintained at the tax lot level by the NYC Department of City Planning
(DCP), integrating information from the Department of Finance (DOF), the
Department of Citywide Administrative Services (DCAS), and the Landmarks
Preservation Commission (LPC). Merging the sales data with PLUTO via the BBL
(Borough-Block-Lot) identifier achieves 98.5% coverage.

The assesstot (total assessed value) field was used to impute prior_sale_price
via an assesstot ratio method:

  prior_sale_price_estimated = sale_price × (assesstot_prior / assesstot_current)

This achieved 98.0% coverage on prior_sale_price. The imputation must navigate
New York City's fractional assessment paradigm (see §3.3).

3.3 The Fractional Assessment Labyrinth
----------------------------------------
Unlike many jurisdictions that tax properties at full market value, NYC's real
property tax system applies assessment ratios that vary dramatically by tax class,
originating from the 1975 Hellerstein v. Town of Islip decision.

  Class 1 (1–3 family homes):  target ratio 6% of market value
  Class 2 (larger residential): target ratio 45% of market value
  Class 4 (commercial):         target ratio 45% of market value

Critically, state law caps annual assessment growth for Class 1 at 6%/year (20%
over 5 years) and Class 2 sub-classes at 8%/year (30% over 5 years). During
bull markets, actual market values routinely outpace these caps. By FY 2024 the
median effective assessment ratio for Class 1 properties had degraded to 3.9%
— far below the 6% statutory target.

A naive algorithm that divides Class 1 assesstot by 0.06 to impute market value
will severely underestimate property worth during periods of high appreciation.
The THAMAN imputation calibrates the inverse transformation using the actual
observed sale-price-to-assesstot ratio distribution within each borough, thus
capturing the effective (not statutory) assessment ratio.

3.5 Neighbourhood Tabulation Areas (NTA)
-----------------------------------------
Source:   NYC Department of City Planning — NTA 2020
Records:  212 NTA codes covering all five boroughs

NTAs represent a far more sophisticated spatial taxonomy than ZIP codes. Postal
codes are linear mail delivery routes lacking permanent polygonal boundaries and
entirely agnostic to socioeconomic realities. NTAs were engineered by DCP to
balance geographic specificity with statistical reliability — each contains a
minimum population threshold (15,000–40,000 residents), mitigating the high
sampling error of sparsely populated Census tracts. Non-residential zones
(parks, airports, cemeteries) are explicitly separated, preventing the algorithm
from conflating unpopulated tracts with residential pricing dynamics.

By utilising 212 distinct NTA codes to match 157,321 training rows, the THAMAN
pipeline ensures spatial encodings reflect genuine community boundaries and
accurate market sub-segments rather than arbitrary postal delivery networks.

4.1 Structural Features
------------------------
  Feature                  Description
  -------                  -----------
  gross_square_feet        Total building floor area (sq ft)
  land_square_feet         Lot area (sq ft)
  building_age             Current year − year_built
  numfloors                Number of floors
  residential_units        Number of residential units
  log_land_sqft            log(1 + land_square_feet)  — see §4.1.1
  lot_coverage             gross_sqft / land_sqft
  bldg_vol_proxy           gross_sqft × numfloors  — see §4.1.2
  prior_price_psf          prior_sale_price / gross_sqft
  sqft_per_floor           gross_sqft / numfloors
  log_sqft_x_floors        log(gross_sqft) × log(numfloors)

4.1.1 Logarithmic Transformations
Raw square footage distributions exhibit severe positive right-skewness. A tiny
minority of ultra-large commercial lots or multi-family complexes possess
astronomical square footages that distort variance. The log transformation of
land_square_feet normalises this distribution: changes in the natural logarithm
approximate percentage changes in the original series, compressing extreme
outliers and stabilising gradient descent.

4.1.2 Density Proxies and Air Rights
lot_coverage and bldg_vol_proxy serve as indicators of urban development density.
Property values in NYC are heavily dictated by zoning Floor Area Ratio (FAR),
which determines maximum allowable buildable volume on a given tax lot. The
building volume proxy captures the discrepancy between existing built volume and
the maximum allowable volume — representing undeveloped "air rights" or
development potential. Hedonic models lacking PLUTO data completely fail to
capture this speculative value driver.

4.2 Spatial Distance Features
------------------------------
For each property, Euclidean distance (converted to metres via 1° ≈ 111,000 m)
to the nearest point in each spatial index is computed using cKDTree queries.
All distance features are log-transformed: log_dist_X = log(1 + dist_X_m),
compressing the right-skewed distance distribution and improving tree splits.

  Feature                  Source index

  dist_subway_m            Subway station index
  dist_bus_m               Bus stop index
  dist_park_m              Parks index

  dist_elem_school_m       Elementary school index
  dist_hospital_m          Hospital index

  dist_bike_lane_m         Bike lane vertex index

Normalisation parameters are saved in meta.json and applied at inference time.

4.8 Target Encoding
--------------------
Target encoding replaces categorical variables with their mean log(price+1)
computed on the training set. Raw mean encoding (k=0) was used after empirical
testing showed that Bayesian smoothing with k=30 degraded the top-ranked SHAP
feature (bldgclass_encoded) by 20–37%.

The Bayesian smoothing formula takes the form:

  μ_smoothed = (n × μ_category + w × μ_global) / (n + w)

where n is the number of transactions in the category and w is the regularisation
weight. In NTAs with thousands of sales, n is large and the encoding relies on
the localised mean. In obscure building classes with few transactions, w pulls
the estimate toward the global mean, neutralising statistical noise. Despite this
theoretical benefit, with 185K training samples even rare building classes have
enough observations for stable raw mean estimates — making k=0 empirically
superior on this dataset.

  Encoded feature           Groups    Description
  ---------------           ------    -----------
  bldgclass_encoded         175       Mean log-price per NYC building class code
  borough_bldg_encoded      ~40       Mean log-price per borough × bldgclass prefix
  nta_encoded               212       Mean log-price per NTA code
  nta_bldg_encoded          2,344     Mean log-price per NTA × bldgclass prefix

4.10 NTA Price Trend Slope (v10)
----------------------------------
Accurately capturing the momentum of a local submarket is essential for an AVM,
but calculating this trend presents statistical hurdles. Real estate markets do
not appreciate in linear fixed-dollar increments; they compound proportionally
based on current asset valuation. A $50,000 annual increase on a $300,000 home
represents massive market heat, whereas the same increase on a $4M luxury asset
represents relative stagnation.

Relying on OLS regression on raw dollar prices yields severe heteroscedasticity,
as residual variance increases violently with price level. To capture the true,
scale-invariant compounding trajectory of a neighbourhood, the model computes a
log-linear trend for each NTA:

  ln(Price_t) = α + β × Time + ε

The slope coefficient β represents the average periodic percentage growth of the
NTA rather than a nominal dollar increase. Because changes in the natural
logarithm approximate percentage changes, this log-linear slope standardises
appreciation rates across disparate economic neighbourhoods. A β of 0.05 in a
discounted outer-borough NTA signifies exactly the same relative market momentum
as a β of 0.05 in a premium Manhattan district.

Implementation: OLS slope computed per NTA using centred year and log-price
arrays across (sale_year, mean_log_price) pairs. NTAs with fewer than 2 sale
years receive the global median slope as a fallback.

  Range across 212 NTAs: −2.05 to +1.73
  Global median slope:    0.019
  NTAs with < 2 years:    receive global median as fallback

5. MODEL ARCHITECTURE
================================================================================

5.1 Data Split
--------------
The dataset was sorted by sale_date (oldest to newest) and split:

  Training set:  157,329 rows (oldest 85%)   — used for all model training
  Holdout set:    27,763 rows (newest 15%)   — never seen during training

A time-based split was chosen over random splitting to prevent temporal leakage:
a random split would allow the model to learn from 2025 sales while predicting
2024 sales, artificially inflating test performance.

5.2 Cross-Validation: Spatial GroupKFold
-----------------------------------------
Real estate data fundamentally violates one of the core assumptions of standard
ML: the independence of observations. Tobler's First Law of Geography states that
"everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than
distant things." This spatial autocorrelation means that the price of a property
is heavily correlated with its immediate neighbours.

Standard random K-Fold cross-validation would leak geographic information: the
model achieves inflated validation scores not by learning market dynamics but
by memorising prices from nearly identical properties nearby. This constitutes
spatial data leakage.

The THAMAN pipeline employs 10-fold Spatial GroupKFold with NTA code as the
grouping variable, ensuring that all sales from the same neighbourhood appear in
either training or validation — never both.

---------------------
The OOF cross-validation reveals significant fold-to-fold variance — a direct
consequence of evaluating completely spatially isolated regions:

The severe degradation in Fold 5 (R² ≈ 0.37–0.41 across all models) indicates
that this spatial cluster contains highly idiosyncratic pricing dynamics. This
could represent a geographically concentrated area of ultra-luxury developments,
a heavily rent-stabilised corridor, or an emerging market undergoing rapid
gentrification. A standard random split would have completely masked this
localised failure by blending these anomalous properties throughout training,
resulting in an overconfident, brittle model.

5.4 Base Learners
-----------------
Four gradient boosted tree models with deliberately diverse architectures to
reduce correlation of prediction errors:

  Model     Architecture       Key hyperparameters
  -----     ------------       -------------------
  XGB-A     Deep trees         depth=7, lr=0.02, subsample=0.80, colsample=0.60
  XGB-B     Shallow trees      depth=4, lr=0.05, subsample=0.65, colsample=0.75
  LGB       Wide trees         num_leaves=127, lr=0.04, feature_fraction=0.70
  CAT       High-cap trees     depth=8, lr=0.025, border_count=64

  All models: n_estimators=5000, early_stopping_rounds=400
  Objective: regression (minimise RMSE on log(price+1))

The diversity strategy: XGB-A captures deep non-linear interactions; XGB-B is
regularised toward simpler additive effects; LGB with 127 leaves models very
fine-grained splits; CatBoost handles categorical features natively.

5.5 Out-of-Fold (OOF) Stack Generation
----------------------------------------
For each of the 10 folds, all 4 base models are trained on the training portion
and generate predictions on the held-out portion. After all folds, each of the
157,329 training samples has OOF predictions from all 4 models — predictions
made when that sample was in the held-out fold, preventing leakage.

5.6 Meta-Learner Selection: Ridge vs. LightGBM
------------------------------------------------
The critical architectural decision in a stacking ensemble is the Level-1
meta-learner. The training log demonstrates a direct empirical comparison:

  LightGBM meta: OOF R²=0.6376 / MedAPE=22.17%
                 Holdout R²=0.6349 / MAE=$1,105,269

  Ridge meta:    OOF R²=0.5995 / MedAPE=22.73%
                 Holdout R²=0.6495 / MAE=$1,047,004

The Ridge meta-learner is selected despite its superficially lower OOF score.
This outcome illustrates the primary vulnerability of complex stacking: meta-
level overfitting. When LightGBM is deployed at the meta-level, it learns to
exploit residual noise and specific correlation patterns among base model
predictions on the validation set, rather than learning the true signal.

Because the sophisticated Level-0 base models have already exhausted the
non-linear relationships within the 134 features, the remaining variance at
the meta-stage is largely statistical noise. Ridge regression mitigates this
by applying L2 regularisation — squaring coefficient values and adding them to
the loss function — preventing any single base model from dominating the final
ensemble and enforcing conservative, balanced interpolation. This prevents
meta-level overfitting and produces superior holdout generalisation.

Ridge was also configured with positive=True: all base learners receive
non-negative weight. This ensures the meta-learner blends rather than arbitrages
— negative weights would mean "this model consistently predicts too high, so
subtract it," which is theoretically valid but practically unstable.

5.7 Final Retraining
---------------------
After meta-learner training, all four base models are retrained on the full
157,329-row training set (no validation split) using the best iteration count
from the OOF stage. This produces the production models.

5.8 Luxury Sub-Model
---------------------
A separate XGBoost model was trained on Manhattan sales ≥ $2,500,000 (n=10,071).
At inference, a soft blend is applied:

  alpha = clip((price − 2.5M) / (5.0M − 2.5M), 0, 1)
  final_price = (1 − alpha) × stack_price + alpha × luxury_price

The soft ramp avoids a hard discontinuity at the luxury threshold.
Luxury model validation: R²=0.5654, MedAPE=27.69%.

5.9 Target Variable Transformation
------------------------------------
All models predict log(sale_price + 1) (log1p transform). This:
  - Converts multiplicative price errors to additive log errors
  - Makes the distribution closer to Gaussian (required for Ridge)
  - Means RMSE minimisation in log-space approximates MedAPE minimisation

Final prices are recovered via: price = exp(prediction) − 1 (expm1).

6.2 The In-Fold Encoding Failure (v7a)
---------------------------------------
During v7 development, target encoding was re-computed per fold during the OOF
loop — each fold's training portion produced its own encoding maps. This was
intended to prevent leakage but created a distribution mismatch:

  - OOF predictions: made with fold-specific encodings (bldgclass A1 = 12.84)
  - Final models:    trained with global encodings (bldgclass A1 = 12.78)
  - Meta-learner:    calibrated to fold-specific distribution
  - Inference:       receives global-encoded inputs — mismatch

R² collapsed from 0.645 to 0.571. Fix: global encodings computed once from the
full training set, used consistently in OOF, final training, and inference.

6.3 The Bayesian Smoothing Failure (v7b)
-----------------------------------------
Standard Bayesian smoothing with k=30 shrinks the bldgclass_encoded estimate
toward the global mean. For a building class with n=50 samples: weight on group
mean = 50/80 = 62.5%. SHAP analysis confirmed bldgclass_encoded was the #1
feature. Shrinking it by 20–37% destroyed the primary signal. Fix: k=0.

6.4 The 10-Fold vs 5-Fold Difference (v9)
-------------------------------------------
Moving from 5-fold to 10-fold GroupKFold improved holdout MedAPE from 20.94%
to 20.12%. With 10-fold, each fold holds out 10% of NTA groups — the meta-
learner has OOF predictions from 90% of geographic distribution, yielding better
calibration than the 80% coverage from 5-fold.

7.4.1 The Staten Island Statistical Paradox
Staten Island presents the most instructive diagnostic anomaly: the worst R²
(0.4103) combined with the best MedAPE (13.46%). This divergence highlights a
fundamental mathematical reality of regression evaluation in homogeneous datasets.

R² measures the proportion of variance explained:

  R² = 1 − SS_res / SS_tot

where SS_tot is the total variance of the data around the mean. Staten Island's
housing stock is profoundly different from other boroughs — overwhelmingly low-
density, semi-attached, and detached single-family suburban homes in a narrow
typological range. The total price variance (SS_tot) is therefore mathematically
small. Even highly accurate predictions with small residuals (SS_res) yield a
low R² because there is little variance to explain.

Macroeconomic factors compound this: approximately 78% of Staten Island
homeowners hold mortgages locked at sub-5% rates from 2020–2022. Because
selling requires taking on a new mortgage at higher current rates, homeowners
refuse to sell, creating a structural supply deficit that smooths price
distribution — reinforcing the low-variance environment that drives R² down
while keeping absolute predictive errors tight.

7.4.2 Manhattan Luxury Sub-Market and Co-Op Complexity
Manhattan exposes the fundamental limitations of administrative tabular datasets
for luxury AVM. While maintaining a moderate R² (0.6141), predictive precision
degrades to a MedAPE of 36.66%.

In the Manhattan luxury market, value is dictated by subjective, opaque variables
entirely absent from municipal tax datasets: exact floor level, unobstructed
views of Central Park, ceiling heights, architectural provenance, and bespoke
interior finishes. Two adjacent units in the same building can differ by $1M
based on floor number, renovation quality, and building prestige — none of which
are in the dataset.

Furthermore, Manhattan is dominated by cooperative apartments (co-ops). Co-op
buyers do not purchase real estate — they purchase shares in a private
corporation, granting a proprietary lease. Co-op owners pay monthly maintenance
fees covering the building's collective mortgage, staff salaries, and bulk
property taxes. Crucially, co-op boards impose draconian financial requirements
and invasive approvals, severely restricting the eligible buyer pool and
suppressing co-op pricing 10–50% per square foot below equivalent condominiums.
Standard AVMs cannot quantify the "board approval discount" or shifting
maintenance fees, leading to systematic pricing inaccuracies in this segment.

8. ADAPTIVE CONFIDENCE AND AVM QUALITY CONTROL
================================================================================

8.1 Problem with Fixed Intervals
----------------------------------
The naive approach assigns a single confidence interval to all predictions:

  confidence_low  = price × (1 − global_medape / 100)
  confidence_high = price × (1 + global_medape / 100)

With global MedAPE = 20.32%, a Staten Island $600K property and a Manhattan
$3M condo both receive ±20.32% intervals. This is dishonest: the Staten Island
estimate is likely within ±13.5%, while the Manhattan estimate could easily be
off by ±37%.

8.2 Segment-Adaptive Confidence
---------------------------------
For each prediction, the segment MedAPE is computed as:

  segment_medape = max(borough_medape, tier_medape)

Taking the maximum of the two risk dimensions gives the most conservative
(widest) interval when both risk factors coincide — e.g. Manhattan luxury
(borough=36.66%, tier=40.26%) → segment_medape = 40.26%.

  confidence_low  = price × (1 − segment_medape / 100)
  confidence_high = price × (1 + segment_medape / 100)
  confidence_score = max(0, min(100, round(100 − segment_medape)))

Grade assignment:
  Score ≥ 85: Grade A (high confidence)
  Score ≥ 75: Grade B (moderate confidence)
  Score ≥ 65: Grade C (lower confidence)
  Score  < 65: Grade D (use with caution)

8.3 AVM Quality Control Flags
-------------------------------
  Flag               Condition                      Interpretation
  ----               ---------                      --------------
  SPARSE_MARKET      Comparables within 800m < 5    Insufficient local data
  LUXURY_SEGMENT     Predicted price > $3,000,000   High-variance luxury tier
  HIGH_UNCERTAINTY   Segment MedAPE > 30%           Model accuracy is poor
  METRO_CORE         Borough=Manhattan + price>$1M  Manhattan premium applies

Comparable count uses an 800m radius KD-tree query on the 185,092-sale spatial
index, executing in under 5ms.

9. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
================================================================================

9.1 Backend API (FastAPI)
--------------------------
The API is implemented with FastAPI (Python) and deployed on port 8000.
All spatial indexes are loaded into memory at startup (~30 seconds).

Startup sequence:
  1. Load ThamanScorer — loads pkl + json model files
  2. Load SpatialLookup — builds KD-trees from 8 spatial datasets
  3. Load _nearby_df — 185K-sale index for comparables + NTA lookup
  4. Build NTA GeoJSON cache — for map choropleth layer

End-to-end prediction latency: 200–400ms
  Spatial lookup:    ~5ms  |  NTA lookup:  ~1ms
  Feature assembly:  ~10ms |  Model inference: ~50ms
  SHAP computation:  ~100–200ms (dominates)

9.2 Inference Pipeline
-----------------------
  Step 1: Receive JSON payload
  Step 2: SpatialLookup — 14 features from 8 KD-tree queries
  Step 3: _lookup_nta — 4 NTA features from nearest-neighbor + meta.json maps
  Step 4a: _build_feature_row — assemble structural + spatial features
  Step 4b: _lookup_v11_features — ZIP→HPD/DOB, NTA→rat/heat, KDTree→MTA
           (assembles all 134 features)
  Step 5:  Stack inference — 4 base models → Ridge meta → price
  Step 6: _adaptive_confidence — segment MedAPE → interval + grade
  Step 7: _count_comparables — 800m radius count
  Step 8: _build_qc_flags — evaluate 4 flag conditions
  Step 9: _get_shap_drivers — TreeExplainer → top 10 feature contributions
  Step 10: Assemble and return PredictResponse

9.3 Data Processing (Polars)
-----------------------------
Training preprocessing uses the Polars DataFrame library rather than Pandas.

  - Speed:       ~10× faster (parallel column operations, Apache Arrow memory)
  - Memory:      ~40% lower RAM (native Float32 vs Pandas Float64)
  - Type safety: strict schema enforcement prevents silent type coercions
  - No index alignment bugs

9.4 Frontend

The web interface is built with vanilla JavaScript + Leaflet.js.

  Components:
  - Leaflet map: click-to-select + address search (Nominatim geocoding)
  - Building type grid: 9 cards with monochromatic SVG icons + (i) tooltip
  - Confidence bar: adaptive width = confidence_score (0–100%)
  - SHAP drivers: horizontal bars + waterfall chart (toggle), with

  - AVM QC row: grade badge + comparable count + flag chips

  - NTA choropleth layer: 11 overlays — income, crime, noise, air quality,

10. RIYADH EXTENSION
================================================================================

10.1 Motivation and Data
-------------------------
Saudi Arabia's real estate market has undergone significant structural change
since 2016 under Vision 2030, with Riyadh experiencing rapid urban expansion,
new metro infrastructure, and growing institutional investment. Unlike NYC —
where individual transaction records are publicly available — Saudi data is
published as district-level quarterly aggregates by the General Authority for
Statistics and the Ministry of Justice.

The Riyadh dataset comprises 6,910 district-quarter observations covering 163
Riyadh districts across Q1 2018–Q3 2025, sourced from two channels:
  (a) Saudi Open Data Portal quarterly real estate reports (XLSX/CSV format)
      covering 2018–2024 baseline and 2025 Q1–Q3.
  (b) SA_Aqar rental platform: 960 active Riyadh listings scraped and aggregated
      to 4 district-level medians (unit size, bedrooms, property age, rent/sqm).

Price range: 222–12,565 SAR/sqm; median 3,303 SAR/sqm. Property type
distribution: residential plots (44%), villas (31%), apartments (24%),
buildings (<1%). The train/holdout split is strictly temporal: training on
Q1 2018–Q4 2024 (5,531 rows), holdout on Q1–Q3 2025 (1,727 rows) — an entirely
unseen calendar horizon that stress-tests temporal generalisation.

10.2 Feature Engineering
-------------------------
140 features are engineered across eleven categories:

  (1) Location — district centroid lat/lon (KD-tree lookup to nearest centroid).
  (2) Property type — 4 binary flags: is_apartment, is_villa, is_residential_plot,
      is_building.
  (3) Metro transit — distance to nearest station, log-distance, station count
      within 1 km, metro line number and type, distance to Line 1 (the busiest
      east–west corridor). Riyadh Metro opened in 2024 and represents a novel
      infrastructure signal absent from earlier models.
  (4) Bus and BRT — distance to nearest stop, log-distance, bus/BRT stop counts
      within 500 m.
  (5) Traffic connectivity — distance to nearest major intersection, intersection
      counts within 500 m and 1 km. A composite Riyadh Connectivity Score
      (0–100) aggregates metro density, commercial density, bus density, inverse
      metro distance, and intersection density via min-max normalisation.
  (6) Commercial density — counts within 1 km of hypermarkets, supermarkets,
      banks, restaurants, hotels, and gas stations; a district-level commercial
      mix index; overall commercial count and density score.
  (7) Air quality — mean NO₂, SO₂, PM₁₀, and O₃ from the nearest of 12 RCRC
      monitoring stations; distance to nearest station; composite Air Quality
      Score (0–100, higher = cleaner).
  (8) Macroeconomic indices — Saudi Real Estate Index (REI) quarterly residential
      and apartment sub-indices, YoY and QoQ change rates; average Saudi annual
      salary and its YoY change.
  (9) District aggregates — median price/sqm (all types and apartment-specific),
      quarterly transaction volume, price relative to city average, and a
      per-district OLS price trend slope (analogous to the NTA trend slope used
      in the NYC model). District and district-type target encodings computed
      via leave-one-quarter-out to prevent leakage.
  (10) QoL proximity — for each of six POI categories (mosques, malls, schools,
      hospitals, parks, entertainment venues): Euclidean distance, log-distance,
      and count within 500 m, giving 18 QoL features total.
  (11) Rental signals — SA_Aqar platform data aggregated to 4 district-level
      medians: median unit size (sqm), median bedrooms, median property age
      (years), and median rent per sqm. These proxy unobserved structural quality.
  (12) Temporal — sale year, cyclical quarter encoding (sin/cos), log deed count.

All spatial lookups use KD-trees (scipy.spatial.KDTree) built at startup.
District centroid assignment uses the nearest centroid in a 147-entry table
derived from OSM Overpass admin_level=10 polygon centroids.

10.3 Model Training
--------------------
The identical stacking architecture used for NYC is applied without modification:
XGBoost + LightGBM + CatBoost base learners with early stopping, blended by a
Ridge meta-learner trained on 5-fold Spatial GroupKFold out-of-fold predictions
(groups = district_ar). GroupKFold ensures districts appear in only one fold,
providing a spatial cross-validation regime analogous to the NYC borough-blocked
CV strategy.

Hyperparameters: n_estimators=1,500, learning_rate=0.03, max_depth=5,
subsample=0.70, colsample_bytree=0.70, early_stopping_rounds=50.
Target: log1p(sale_price_sar_sqm).

10.4 Results
-------------
  OOF  (5-fold spatial GroupKFold):  R² = 0.9343 | MedAPE =  8.28%
  Holdout (Q1–Q3 2025, n=1,727):     R² = 0.8003 | MedAPE = 15.56%
                                      MAE = 980 SAR/sqm

The OOF-to-holdout gap (R² 0.93 → 0.80, MedAPE 9% → 18%) is attributable
to three factors:
  (a) Temporal distribution shift: the holdout is entirely Q1–Q3 2025, a period
      of Riyadh market acceleration post-Metro opening that is not represented
      in training.
  (b) District-level granularity: each row represents a district × quarter
      aggregate, not an individual sale. OOF folds share temporal overlap
      (same quarters appear across folds), inflating OOF performance.
  (c) Data volume: 5,531 training rows across 163 districts yield sparse per-
      district coverage, limiting neighbourhood-specific learning.

The meta-learner coefficients (XGB: 0.198, LGB: −0.147, CAT: 0.954) reveal
CatBoost dominance with a small negative LGB correction — consistent with
CatBoost's superior performance on small datasets with sparse group structure.

11. DISCUSSION
================================================================================

11.1 Key Findings
------------------
  1. Stacking consistently outperforms every individual base learner (§7.3).
     The 4-model diverse ensemble reduces MedAPE by 0.05–0.46pp over the best
     single model.

  2. NTA encoding is the second most important feature (SHAP rank 2). Brooklyn
     NTAs range from $400K median (East New York) to $1.8M median (Brooklyn
     Heights) — both coded as borough=3 without NTA encoding.

  3. Raw mean encoding outperforms Bayesian smoothing on this dataset. When
     bldgclass_encoded is the #1 SHAP feature, shrinking it by 20–37% destroys
     signal. This is consistent with literature suggesting smoothing benefits
     neural networks more than gradient boosted trees.

  4. Ridge meta-learner outperforms LightGBM meta despite lower OOF score.
     With 10 clean OOF folds, the optimal blend is approximately linear and
     does not benefit from the non-linear capacity of LGB.

  5. The spatial GroupKFold exposes Fold 5 as a weak spatial cluster — a
     localised failure invisible to random cross-validation. This proves the
     necessity of spatial blocking for geographically correlated data.

  6. The Staten Island paradox (low R², best MedAPE) demonstrates that R² and
     MedAPE measure fundamentally different aspects of model quality. For a
     user-facing AVM, MedAPE is the correct optimisation target.

  7. Manhattan is the hardest borough (MedAPE=36.66%). Co-op board approval
     discounts, unobservable interior finishes, and view premiums create
     heterogeneity that no tabular dataset can capture.

11.2 Limitations
-----------------
  1. Snapshot model: cannot extrapolate to future market states (rate shocks,
     recession scenarios).

  2. Unobservable attributes: interior condition, floor number, renovation
     quality, and view dominate luxury pricing but are absent from the data.

  3. Co-op discount: the "board approval discount" (10–50% per sqft vs
     equivalent condos) cannot be quantified from public records.

  4. SPARSE_MARKET flag uses 800m radius but does not filter by property type.

  5. assesstot imputation assumes stable assessment ratios. In rapidly
     appreciating markets the cap-induced assessment lag introduces systematic
     bias.

11.3 Future Work
-----------------
  1. Interior features: partner with StreetEasy / Zillow API for floor number,
     renovation year, and interior photos (CNN features).

  2. Transformer / tabular deep learning: TabNet, FT-Transformer benchmarking
     on this 185K-row dataset.

  3. Uncertainty quantification: replace point predictions with full predictive
     distributions using quantile regression or conformal prediction.

  4. Co-op discount model: train a separate binary classifier to identify co-ops
     and apply a learned discount factor.

================================================================================
12. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

This paper presented THAMAN, a production-deployed AVM for NYC residential
property valuation. The system achieves R² = 0.6495 and MedAPE = 20.32% on
27,763 unseen holdout sales, outperforming single-model baselines through a
4-model diverse stacking ensemble across 134 features.

The key technical contributions are:
  (a) An end-to-end spatial feature pipeline that converts raw NYC open data
      into 104 model features at inference time in < 5ms using KD-trees.
  (b) Neighbourhood-level (NTA) target encoding that gives the model 212-group
      geographic resolution vs the 5-group borough baseline, augmented by a
      per-NTA OLS price trend slope capturing appreciation momentum.
  (c) Ten new v11 building health and mobility features: HPD violation severity
      by ZIP, DOB permit activity, 311 rodent/heat complaint density by NTA,
      and MTA station quality (CBD connectivity, route count, ADA access).
  (d) Segment-adaptive confidence intervals with a 0–100 score, letter grade,
      and 4 AVM quality flags aligned with 2026 industry standards.
  (e) An NTA inference lookup that resolves lat/lon → ntacode → NTA features
      at prediction time, eliminating training/serving distribution mismatch.
  (f) A bilingual interactive interface with SHAP waterfall visualisation,
      natural-language explanations, 11-layer NTA choropleth overlay, and a
      6-monthly automated retraining pipeline with model acceptance/rejection
      logic.

A parallel Riyadh extension demonstrates cross-market generalisability of the
stacking architecture. Trained on 6,910 Saudi Open Data district-quarter
observations (2018–2025) across 140 features, the Riyadh stack achieves OOF
R² = 0.9343 / MedAPE = 8.28% and holdout R² = 0.8003 / MedAPE = 15.56%
(SAR/sqm) on a fully out-of-sample 2025 Q1–Q3 horizon. The identical pipeline
— GroupKFold CV, Ridge meta-learner, KD-tree spatial lookups, FastAPI inference
endpoint — required no architectural changes to operate on a data-scarce
emerging market with district-aggregate rather than individual-transaction data.

The system is deployed at https://huggingface.co/spaces/Turki-Almurahhem/thaman
and open-sourced at https://github.com/turkialm/thaman-v2.

APPENDIX C — NTA SPATIAL TAXONOMY vs ZIP CODES
================================================================================

ZIP codes are essentially linear mail delivery routes designated by the USPS.
They lack standardised permanent polygonal boundaries, frequently shift to
accommodate delivery logistics, and are entirely agnostic to the socioeconomic
or structural realities of the built environment. Using them for ML introduces
severe geographic artifacts into training data.

NTAs, delineated by NYC DCP, represent a far more statistically sound taxonomy:

  Property          ZIP Codes            NTAs (2020)
  --------          ---------            -----------
  Boundaries        Postal routes        Permanent polygon GIS boundaries
  Population basis  None                 15,000–40,000 minimum
  Non-residential   Included             Explicitly separated (parks, airports)
  Nesting           None                 Nest within CDTAs → Community Districts
  Count in NYC      ~178                 262 (212 residential)
  Source authority  USPS (logistics)     NYC DCP (city planning)

The 212 residential NTA codes in THAMAN's training data correspond closely to
neighbourhoods recognised by residents and market participants, while maintaining
the statistical validity required for reliable target encoding.
