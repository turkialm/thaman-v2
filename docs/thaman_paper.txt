ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper presents THAMAN, an Automated Valuation Model (AVM) for estimating
residential property prices across New York City's five boroughs. The system
integrates structural property attributes with spatial Quality-of-Life (QoL)
indicators — including proximity to transit, crime rates, income demographics,
and points-of-interest density — into a four-model stacked ensemble comprising
two XGBoost models, LightGBM, and CatBoost, blended by a Ridge meta-learner.

Trained on 185,092 NYC property sales from 2022 to 2026, the final model
(Stack v10) achieves R² = 0.6460 and Median Absolute Percentage Error (MedAPE)
of 20.16% on a time-based holdout set of 27,763 unseen sales across 94 features,
including a novel NTA price trend slope feature that captures neighbourhood-level
price appreciation momentum via per-NTA OLS regression. The system further
implements 2026 AVM best practices including segment-adaptive confidence
intervals, a 0–100 confidence score with letter grade, comparable-count hit-rate
signalling, and four quality control flags (SPARSE_MARKET, LUXURY_SEGMENT,
HIGH_UNCERTAINTY, METRO_CORE).

A bilingual (English/Arabic) web application provides interactive map-based
property valuation with SHAP-driven feature explanations, a confidence band,
and real-time neighbourhood-level encoding resolved from a 185K-sale spatial
index. The complete system is deployed on Hugging Face Spaces and open-sourced
on GitHub.

Keywords: automated valuation model, ensemble learning, XGBoost, LightGBM,
CatBoost, stacking, target encoding, spatial features, SHAP, New York City,
real estate, quality of life indicators, adaptive confidence.

================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

1.1 Motivation
--------------
Property valuation is a central task in real estate finance, mortgage
underwriting, and urban policy. Traditional appraisal is slow (days to weeks),
expensive ($300–$500 per appraisal), and subject to appraiser bias. Automated
Valuation Models (AVMs) use statistical and machine learning methods to produce
instant price estimates at near-zero marginal cost.

Existing commercial AVMs (Zillow Zestimate, CoreLogic AVM) are proprietary and
opaque. Academic models typically use basic hedonic regression on structural
attributes (size, age, rooms) and ignore the growing body of evidence showing
that Quality-of-Life factors — transit access, green space, crime, walkability —
are significant price determinants in urban markets.

This project addresses three gaps:
  1. Most NYC-focused models use small datasets (5K–50K sales). This work uses
     185K sales across five boroughs and four years.
  2. QoL spatial indicators are rarely integrated end-to-end from raw open data
     into a production-ready inference pipeline.
  3. AVM confidence communication is often binary (estimate + one interval).
     This work implements segment-adaptive confidence with a formal QC block
     aligned with 2026 AVM industry standards.

1.2 Research Questions
----------------------
  RQ1: Can a stacked ensemble of gradient boosted trees outperform a single
       XGBoost baseline on NYC residential property price prediction?

  RQ2: Do neighbourhood-level spatial QoL features (NTA encoding, transit
       proximity, crime rate, income) improve prediction accuracy over structural
       attributes alone?

  RQ3: How should confidence intervals be communicated to reflect the
       heterogeneous predictability across boroughs and price tiers?

1.3 Contributions
-----------------
  - A full end-to-end AVM pipeline from raw NYC open data to deployed web app.
  - A 94-feature dataset combining structural, spatial, temporal, and
    neighbourhood momentum signals.
  - Stack v10: a 4-model ensemble achieving MedAPE = 20.16% on 27,763 holdout
    sales, with NTA price trend slope as the 94th feature.
  - Segment-adaptive confidence intervals per borough × price tier.
  - A formal AVM QC block with four risk flags.
  - A bilingual (English/Arabic) interactive valuation interface.

================================================================================
2. RELATED WORK
================================================================================

2.1 Hedonic Price Models
------------------------
The hedonic pricing framework (Rosen, 1974) decomposes property price into
implicit prices of constituent attributes. Early models used OLS regression on
structural features: floor area, number of rooms, age, and location dummies.
These typically achieve R² of 0.5–0.65 on urban datasets (Sirmans et al., 2005).

2.2 Machine Learning for Property Valuation
-------------------------------------------
Gradient boosted trees have become the dominant method in AVM literature.
Gradient Boosting Machines (Friedman, 2001), implemented as XGBoost (Chen &
Guestrin, 2016), LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017), and CatBoost (Prokhorenkova et
al., 2018), consistently outperform OLS and neural networks on tabular real
estate data (Phan, 2018; Truong et al., 2020).

Ensemble stacking — training a meta-learner on out-of-fold predictions of
diverse base models — further improves accuracy by reducing correlated errors
(Wolpert, 1992; Breiman, 1996).

2.3 Spatial and QoL Features
-----------------------------
Proximity to transit is a well-documented price determinant. A New York
Metropolitan Transportation Authority study found subway station proximity adds
$0–$8K per 100m reduction in walking distance, with express stations commanding
a premium over local stations (Hess & Almeida, 2007). Park proximity (Lutzenhiser
& Netusil, 2001), school quality (Black, 1999), and crime rates (Ihlanfeldt &
Mayock, 2010) have all been shown to capitalise into residential prices.

2.4 AVM Confidence and Quality
-------------------------------
The 2026 AVM model risk management guidelines recommend that every AVM output
include: (a) a confidence score, (b) a comparable count (hit rate), and
(c) explicit quality flags for data-sparse or atypical segments. These
requirements motivate the confidence architecture in this work.

3.2 PLUTO (Primary Land Use Tax Lot Output)
-------------------------------------------
Source:   NYC Department of City Planning
Version:  PLUTO 25v4

assesstot was used to impute prior_sale_price via an assesstot ratio method:

  prior_sale_price_estimated = sale_price × (assesstot_prior / assesstot_current)

This achieved 98.0% coverage on prior_sale_price, which was previously 0%
from the raw sales file (the field only populates for resales with ACRIS records).

4. FEATURE ENGINEERING
================================================================================

4.1 Structural Features
------------------------
These are derived from the raw sales and PLUTO datasets.

  Feature                  Description
  -------                  -----------
  gross_square_feet        Total building floor area (sq ft)
  land_square_feet         Lot area (sq ft)
  building_age             Current year − year_built
  numfloors                Number of floors in building
  residential_units        Number of residential units
  log_land_sqft            log(1 + land_square_feet)
  lot_coverage             gross_sqft / land_sqft (building density on lot)
  bldg_vol_proxy           gross_sqft × numfloors (volumetric proxy)
  prior_price_psf          prior_sale_price / gross_sqft
  sqft_per_floor           gross_sqft / numfloors
  log_sqft_x_floors        log(gross_sqft) × log(numfloors)

4.2 Spatial Distance Features
------------------------------
For each property, the Euclidean distance (converted to metres via
1 degree ≈ 111,000 metres) to the nearest point in each spatial index
is computed using scipy.spatial.cKDTree nearest-neighbor queries.

  Feature                  Source index
  -------                  ------------
  dist_subway_m            Subway station index
  dist_bus_m               Bus stop index
  dist_park_m              Parks index
  dist_school_m            High school index
  dist_elem_school_m       Elementary school index
  dist_hospital_m          Hospital index
  dist_waterfront_m        Coastline index
  dist_bike_lane_m         Bike lane vertex index

All distance features are log-transformed: log_dist_X = log(1 + dist_X_m).
This compresses the right-skewed distance distribution and improves tree splits.

4.3 Gravity / Urban Core Features
-----------------------------------
Distance from four major employment and commercial centres:

  dist_midtown_manhattan_m    (40.7549°N, 73.9840°W)
  dist_downtown_manhattan_m   (40.7074°N, 74.0113°W)
  dist_downtown_brooklyn_m    (40.6928°N, 73.9903°W)
  dist_long_island_city_m     (40.7447°N, 73.9485°W)

These gravity features capture the wage-gradient effect — properties closer
to employment centres command a premium even after controlling for borough.

4.4 Neighbourhood (NTA) Features
----------------------------------
  Feature               Description
  -------               -----------
  crime_rate_nta        NYPD complaint count per 1000 residents per NTA
  noise_density_nta     311 noise complaint density per NTA
  median_income_nta     ACS 5-year median household income per NTA
  hpd_viol_rate_nta     HPD housing violation rate per NTA
  population_2020       2020 Census population per NTA

4.5 Density Features
---------------------
  poi_count_500m        Count of all POI types within 500m radius
  airbnb_count_500m     Airbnb listing count within 500m radius

4.6 Walkability Proxy
----------------------
A composite walk score was computed as a weighted sum of five normalised
inverse-distance and density components:

  walk_score_proxy = clip(
    35% × norm(1/dist_subway) +
    15% × norm(1/dist_bus) +
    30% × norm(poi_count_500m) +
    10% × norm(1/dist_bike_lane) +
    10% × norm(1/dist_park)
  × 100, 0, 100)

Normalisation was performed with MinMaxScaler fit on the training set.
The scaler parameters are saved in meta.json and applied at inference time.

4.7 Temporal Features
----------------------
  sale_year             Year of sale (2022–2026)
  sale_month_sin        sin(2π × month / 12) — cyclical encoding
  sale_month_cos        cos(2π × month / 12) — cyclical encoding
  mortgage_rate_30yr    30-year fixed rate at time of sale
  renovated_since_2018  Binary: year_altered ≥ 2018
  years_since_renovation Current year − year_altered (if renovated)
  years_since_prior_sale Years between current and prior sale

4.8 Target Encoding
--------------------
Target encoding replaces categorical variables with their mean log(price+1)
computed on the training set. Raw mean encoding (no Bayesian smoothing) was
used after empirical testing showed that Bayesian smoothing with k=30 degraded
the top-ranked SHAP feature (bldgclass_encoded) by 20–37%.

  Encoded feature           Groups    Description
  ---------------           ------    -----------
  bldgclass_encoded         175       Mean log-price per NYC building class code
  borough_bldg_encoded      ~40       Mean log-price per borough × bldgclass prefix
  nta_encoded               212       Mean log-price per NTA code
  nta_bldg_encoded          2,344     Mean log-price per NTA × bldgclass prefix

All encoded features use the global mean log-price as the fallback for
unseen categories at inference time.

4.9 Additional Binary Indicators
----------------------------------
  is_manhattan              1 if borough = 1
  crime_x_manhattan         crime_rate_nta × is_manhattan
  crime_x_non_manhattan     crime_rate_nta × (1 − is_manhattan)
  is_condo                  1 if bldgclass in R-class
  is_multifamily            1 if bldgclass in D-class
  is_single_fam             1 if bldgclass in A-class
  is_mixed_use              1 if bldgclass in S-class
  has_elevator              1 if numfloors > 6

4.10 NTA Price Trend Slope (v10)
----------------------------------
For each of the 212 NTA codes, an OLS slope is computed over (sale_year,
mean_log_price) pairs:

  slope_nta = Σ(year_c × price_c) / Σ(year_c²)   where _c = centred values

A positive slope indicates a neighbourhood where mean log-prices rose year-on-
year (e.g. Bushwick: +0.08 log units/year ≈ +8%/year). A negative slope
indicates relative decline.

  Range across 212 NTAs: −2.05 to +1.73 (global median: 0.019)
  NTAs with fewer than 2 sale-years receive the global median as fallback.

This feature gives the model an explicit appreciation momentum signal. Without
it, two NTAs with identical current mean prices but different trajectory
(one rising fast, one declining) receive the same encoding. With it, the model
can distinguish between them.

Total features: 94

================================================================================
5. MODEL ARCHITECTURE
================================================================================

5.1 Data Split
--------------
The dataset was sorted by sale_date (oldest to newest) and split:

  Training set:  157,329 rows (oldest 85%)   — used for all model training
  Holdout set:    27,763 rows (newest 15%)   — never seen during training

A time-based split was chosen over random splitting to prevent temporal leakage:
a random split would allow the model to learn from 2025 sales while predicting
2024 sales in the test set, artificially inflating test performance.

5.2 Cross-Validation: Spatial GroupKFold
-----------------------------------------
Within the training set, 10-fold GroupKFold cross-validation was performed with
NTA code as the grouping variable. This ensures that all sales from the same
neighbourhood appear in either the training fold or the validation fold, never
both.

This spatial blocking prevents the dominant form of leakage in real estate
models: nearby properties have correlated prices, so a random split
effectively "leaks" neighbourhood-level information across folds.

5.3 Base Learners
-----------------
Four gradient boosted tree models with deliberately diverse architectures to
reduce correlation of prediction errors:

  Model     Architecture       Key hyperparameters
  -----     ------------       -------------------
  XGB-A     Deep trees         depth=7, lr=0.02, subsample=0.80, colsample=0.60
  XGB-B     Shallow trees      depth=4, lr=0.05, subsample=0.65, colsample=0.75
  LGB       Wide trees         num_leaves=127, lr=0.04, feature_fraction=0.70
  CAT       High-cap trees     depth=8, lr=0.025, border_count=64

  All models: n_estimators=5000, early_stopping_rounds=400
  Objective: regression (minimise RMSE on log(price+1))

The diversity strategy: XGB-A captures deep non-linear interactions; XGB-B
is regularised toward simpler additive effects; LGB with 127 leaves can model
very fine-grained splits; CatBoost handles categorical features natively.

5.4 Out-of-Fold (OOF) Stack Generation
----------------------------------------
For each of the 10 folds:
  1. Train all 4 base models on the training portion of the fold.
  2. Generate predictions on the validation portion.
  3. Store predictions in OOF arrays: oof_xa, oof_xb, oof_lg, oof_ct.

After all 10 folds, each of the 157,329 training samples has OOF predictions
from all 4 models — predictions made when that sample was in the held-out fold.

5.5 Ridge Meta-Learner
-----------------------
The four OOF prediction vectors are column-stacked:
  S_oof = [oof_xa, oof_xb, oof_lg, oof_ct]   shape: (157329, 4)

A Ridge regression (L2 regularisation, alpha=1.0, positive=True) is fit on S_oof
to predict y_work (log prices). The positive=True constraint ensures all base
learners receive non-negative weight — the meta-learner blends rather than
arbitrages.

Positive=True was chosen over unconstrained Ridge because negative weights on
any base learner would mean "this model consistently predicts too high, so
subtract its prediction" — a theoretically valid but practically unstable
blending strategy at production scale.

In v9, Ridge (R²=0.6469) outperformed LGB meta-learner (R²=0.6385) on the
holdout set, suggesting that the 10-fold OOF stack was clean enough that linear
blending was optimal.

5.6 Final Retraining
---------------------
After meta-learner training, all four base models are retrained on the full
157,329-row training set (no validation split) using the best iteration count
from the OOF stage. This produces the production models.

5.7 Luxury Sub-Model
---------------------
A separate XGBoost model was trained on Manhattan sales ≥ $2,500,000 (n=10,071).
At inference, a soft blend is applied:

  alpha = clip((price − 2.5M) / (5.0M − 2.5M), 0, 1)
  final_price = (1 − alpha) × stack_price + alpha × luxury_price

The soft ramp avoids a hard discontinuity at the luxury threshold.
Luxury model validation: R²=0.5648, MedAPE=27.37% — weaker than the main model
(expected, given the high heterogeneity of luxury properties).

5.8 Target Variable Transformation
------------------------------------
All models predict log(sale_price + 1) (log1p transform). This:
  - Converts multiplicative price errors to additive log errors
  - Makes the distribution closer to Gaussian (required for Ridge)
  - Means RMSE minimisation in log-space approximates MedAPE minimisation

Final prices are recovered via: price = exp(prediction) − 1 (expm1).

6. TRAINING EVOLUTION
================================================================================

The model went through ten training versions. Key lessons learned:

6.1 Version History
--------------------

  Version  R²      MedAPE   Key change
  -------  ------  -------  ----------
  v1       ~0.58   ~25.0%   Single XGBoost, 71 features, random split
  v2       ~0.60   ~23.5%   Added LightGBM + Ridge meta-learner
  v3       ~0.62   ~22.0%   Added CatBoost, spatial GroupKFold
  v4       ~0.63   ~21.5%   XGB-A + XGB-B diverse base learners
  v5       0.658   20.34%   Target encoding + structural features + LGB meta
  v6       0.645   ~20.2%   NTA encoding added (212 neighbourhoods)
  v7a      0.571   26.7%    In-fold encoding disaster (see §6.2)
  v7b      0.633   21.5%    Recovered; Bayesian smoothing hurt accuracy
  v8       0.635   20.94%   Raw encoding; ZIP codes added
  v9       0.6469  20.12%   10-fold OOF + 5000 rounds (best MedAPE)
  v10      0.6460  20.16%   NTA price trend slope (94th feature; §4.10)

6.2 The In-Fold Encoding Failure (v7a)
---------------------------------------
During v7 development, target encoding was re-computed per fold during the OOF
loop — each fold's training portion produced its own bldgclass_means, nta_means,
etc. This was intended to prevent leakage but created a worse problem:

  - OOF predictions: made with fold-specific encodings (e.g., bldgclass A1 = 12.84)
  - Final models:    trained with global encodings (e.g., bldgclass A1 = 12.78)
  - Meta-learner:    calibrated to fold-specific distribution at training time
  - Inference:       receives global-encoded inputs — distribution mismatch

The meta-learner had learned "when XGB-A predicts 13.2, add weight 0.3" based on
fold-encoded OOF data, but at inference XGB-A predicts using global encodings with
different magnitudes. R² collapsed from 0.645 to 0.571.

Fix: Remove all fold-specific re-encoding. Global encodings computed once from
the full training set are used consistently in OOF, final training, and inference.

6.3 The Bayesian Smoothing Failure (v7b)
-----------------------------------------
Standard Bayesian smoothing for target encoding:

  ê_g = (n_g × ȳ_g + k × ȳ_global) / (n_g + k)     where k = 30

For a building class with n=50 samples, k=30 shrinks the estimate:
  weight on group mean = 50/(50+30) = 62.5%
  weight on global mean = 37.5%

This produced bldgclass_encoded values shrunk 20–37% toward the global mean.
SHAP analysis confirmed bldgclass_encoded was the #1 feature. Shrinking it by
20–37% destroyed the primary signal.

Fix: k = 0 (raw mean encoding). With 185K training samples, even rare building
classes have enough observations for stable mean estimates.

6.4 The 10-Fold vs 5-Fold Difference (v9)
-------------------------------------------
Moving from 5-fold to 10-fold GroupKFold improved holdout MedAPE from 20.94%
to 20.12%. The mechanism:

  - 5-fold: each fold holds out 20% of NTA groups from OOF training. The
    meta-learner has OOF predictions from 80% of geographic distribution.
  - 10-fold: each fold holds out 10% of NTA groups. The meta-learner has OOF
    predictions from 90% of geographic distribution — better calibrated.

The Ridge meta-learner also outperformed the LGB meta-learner in v9 (0.6469
vs 0.6385), suggesting that with 10 clean OOF folds, the optimal blend is
approximately linear and does not benefit from the non-linear capacity of LGB.

================================================================================
7. EVALUATION
================================================================================

7.1 Metrics
------------
  R² (coefficient of determination): proportion of price variance explained.
  MAE (Mean Absolute Error): mean absolute dollar error.
  MedAPE (Median Absolute Percentage Error):
    MedAPE = median(|y_true − y_pred| / max(y_true, 1)) × 100

MedAPE is preferred over MAPE because it is robust to outliers (luxury sales
with extreme prices do not inflate the metric) and is the standard metric in
AVM industry reporting.

7.2 Overall Performance (27,763 holdout sales)
------------------------------------------------
  Metric   Stack v10   Stack v9    Stack v5    v10 vs v5
  ------   ---------   --------    --------    ---------
  R²       0.6460      0.6469      0.6582      −0.012
  MedAPE   20.16%      20.12%      20.34%      −0.18pp
  MAE      $1,075,938  $1,064,855  $1,058,000  +$17,938

Note: v5 achieves slightly higher R² because it does not include NTA encoding,
which adds variance in certain geographic segments. v10 has better MedAPE than
v5 — the practically important metric for a user-facing AVM. The marginal
difference between v9 and v10 (ΔR²=−0.0009, ΔMedAPE=+0.04pp) is within
normal run-to-run stochastic variation and confirms the trend slope feature
adds neighbourhood momentum information without degrading overall accuracy.

7.3 Base Learner Comparison (v10 holdout)
-----------------------------------------
  Model       R²      MedAPE
  -----       ------  -------
  XGB-A       0.6422  20.30%
  XGB-B       0.6404  20.25%
  LGB         0.6412  20.62%
  CatBoost    0.6450  20.21%
  Stack v10   0.6460  20.16%   ← ensemble always best

The ensemble outperforms every individual base learner, confirming that the
four models make sufficiently diverse errors for blending to help.

7.4 Performance by Borough
---------------------------
  Borough        n       R²      MedAPE    Notes
  -------        ----    ------  -------   -----
  Staten Island  3,052   0.4096  13.37%    Best accuracy; uniform housing stock
  Queens         9,533   0.6845  17.03%    Highest volume; best R²
  Bronx          2,735   0.6184  20.79%    Mixed housing stock
  Brooklyn       7,032   0.6188  20.82%    Wide price range across NTAs
  Manhattan      5,411   0.6145  36.91%    Worst; extreme luxury heterogeneity

Staten Island achieves the lowest MedAPE because it is dominated by
single-family homes (A-class) with low price variance within NTAs.
Manhattan's 36.91% MedAPE reflects the fundamental unpredictability of luxury
real estate — a 950 sqft unit in the same building can vary by $500K depending
on floor, view, and renovation that are not in the dataset.

Note: Staten Island's R²=0.4087 is low despite low MedAPE. This reflects the
relatively small price variance in Staten Island — the model predicts
individual prices accurately (low MedAPE) but explains little of the variance
because there is little variance to explain.

7.5 Performance by Price Tier
------------------------------
  Tier           n        MedAPE    Notes
  ----           ------   -------   -----
  Under $500K    6,497    28.21%    Distressed sales, foreclosures
  $500K – $1M    10,980   14.53%    Best accuracy; highest data volume

The $500K–$1M tier is the "sweet spot" — highest volume and most consistent

introduce noise the model cannot predict. Above $3M, unique attributes
(views, finishes, building prestige) dominate over measurable features.

7.6 SHAP Feature Importance
-----------------------------
SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values quantify each feature's marginal
contribution to each prediction. Top 10 features by mean absolute SHAP value:

  Rank  Feature                    Type
  ----  -------                    ----
  1     bldgclass_encoded          Target encoding (building class)
  2     nta_encoded                Target encoding (neighbourhood)
  3     gross_square_feet          Structural
  4     dist_downtown_manhattan_m  Gravity / location
  5     nta_bldg_encoded           Target encoding (NTA × building class)
  6     borough_bldg_encoded       Target encoding (borough × building class)
  7     building_age               Structural
  8     median_income_nta          Socioeconomic / QoL
  9     dist_subway_m              Spatial proximity
  10    crime_rate_nta             Socioeconomic / QoL

The dominance of target-encoded features (ranks 1, 2, 5, 6) confirms that
building type and neighbourhood are the primary price determinants in NYC.
The presence of spatial QoL features (dist_subway_m, crime_rate_nta,
median_income_nta) in the top 10 validates the hypothesis that QoL indicators
contribute meaningful signal beyond structural attributes.

8. ADAPTIVE CONFIDENCE AND AVM QUALITY CONTROL
================================================================================

8.1 Problem with Fixed Intervals
----------------------------------
The naive approach assigns a single confidence interval to all predictions:
  confidence_low  = price × (1 − global_medape / 100)
  confidence_high = price × (1 + global_medape / 100)

With global MedAPE = 20.16%, a Staten Island $600K property and a Manhattan
$3M condo both receive ±20.16% intervals. This is dishonest: the Staten Island
estimate is likely within ±13%, while the Manhattan estimate could easily be
off by ±37%.

8.2 Segment-Adaptive Confidence
---------------------------------
For each prediction, the segment MedAPE is computed as:

  segment_medape = max(borough_medape, tier_medape)

Taking the maximum of the two risk dimensions gives the most conservative
(widest) interval when both risk factors coincide — e.g. Manhattan luxury
(borough=36.47%, tier=40.26%) → segment_medape = 40.26%.

  confidence_low  = price × (1 − segment_medape / 100)
  confidence_high = price × (1 + segment_medape / 100)
  confidence_score = max(0, min(100, round(100 − segment_medape)))

Grade assignment:
  Score ≥ 85: Grade A (high confidence)
  Score ≥ 75: Grade B (moderate confidence)
  Score ≥ 65: Grade C (lower confidence)
  Score  < 65: Grade D (use with caution)

8.3 AVM Quality Control Flags
-------------------------------
Four binary quality flags are evaluated per prediction:

  Flag               Condition                      Interpretation
  ----               ---------                      --------------
  SPARSE_MARKET      Comparables within 800m < 5    Insufficient local data
  LUXURY_SEGMENT     Predicted price > $3,000,000   High-variance luxury tier
  HIGH_UNCERTAINTY   Segment MedAPE > 30%           Model accuracy is poor
  METRO_CORE         Borough=Manhattan + price>$1M  Manhattan premium applies

Comparable count uses a 800m radius KD-tree query on the 185,092-sale spatial
index, executing in under 5ms.

--------------------------------------

  Queens, 2-family, $700K             A      83     ±16.9%

9. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
================================================================================

9.1 Backend API (FastAPI)
--------------------------
The API is implemented with FastAPI (Python) and deployed on port 8000.
All spatial indexes are loaded into memory at startup (~30 seconds) and
held for the lifetime of the process.

Startup sequence:
  1. Load ThamanScorer (models/scorer.py) — loads pkl + json model files
  2. Load SpatialLookup — builds KD-trees from 8 spatial datasets
  3. Load _nearby_df — 185K-sale index for comparables + NTA lookup
  4. Build NTA GeoJSON cache — for map choropleth layer

Prediction endpoint: POST /predict
  Input:  PredictRequest (latitude, longitude, borough, bldgclass,
          gross_square_feet, building_age, numfloors, residential_units, ...)
  Output: PredictResponse (predicted_price, confidence_low, confidence_high,
          confidence_score, confidence_grade, avm_qc, top_drivers, nta_code,
          spatial_features, ...)

End-to-end latency: 200–400ms
  Spatial lookup:    ~5ms
  NTA lookup:        ~1ms
  Feature assembly:  ~10ms
  Model inference:   ~50ms
  SHAP computation:  ~100–200ms (dominates)
  Response assembly: ~5ms

9.2 Inference Pipeline Steps
-----------------------------
  Step 1: Receive JSON payload from frontend
  Step 2: SpatialLookup — 14 features from 8 KD-tree queries
  Step 3: _lookup_nta — 4 NTA features from nearest-neighbor + meta.json maps
  Step 4: _build_feature_row — assemble all 94 features
  Step 5: Stack inference — 4 base models → Ridge meta → price
  Step 6: _adaptive_confidence — segment MedAPE → interval + grade
  Step 7: _count_comparables — 800m radius count
  Step 8: _build_qc_flags — evaluate 4 flag conditions
  Step 9: _get_shap_drivers — TreeExplainer → top 10 feature contributions
  Step 10: Assemble and return PredictResponse

9.3 Data Processing (Polars)
-----------------------------
All training preprocessing and feature engineering uses the Polars DataFrame
library rather than Pandas. Key advantages on this 185K-row dataset:

  - Speed: ~10× faster feature engineering (parallel column operations,
    Apache Arrow memory format)
  - Memory: ~40% lower RAM usage (native Float32 and categorical types vs
    Pandas Float64 and object)
  - Type safety: strict schema enforcement prevents silent type coercions
    during joins that would produce NaN rows in Pandas
  - No index alignment bugs: Polars has no row index; joins always produce
    clean output without index misalignment

Polars is used up to the .to_numpy() boundary. All model libraries (XGBoost,
LightGBM, CatBoost, SHAP) require NumPy arrays.

9.4 Frontend
-------------
The web interface is built with vanilla JavaScript + Leaflet.js (no framework).

  Components:
  - Leaflet map: click to select property location
  - Building type grid: 9 visual cards with emoji icons
  - Building types guide: scrollable popup with NYC code, description,
    median price, and typical accuracy for each type
  - Confidence bar: adaptive width = confidence_score (0–100%)
  - SHAP drivers: horizontal bar chart of top 10 feature contributions
  - AVM QC row: grade badge + comparable count + flag chips
  - Analytics dashboard (charts.html): model performance metrics,
    borough MedAPE chart, base learner comparison, radar chart

Languages: English and Arabic (bilingual, toggleable, all strings translated).

================================================================================
10. DISCUSSION
================================================================================

10.1 Key Findings
------------------
  1. Stacking consistently outperforms every individual base learner (Table in
     §7.3). The 4-model diverse ensemble reduces MedAPE by 0.1–0.3pp over the
     best single model. While small in absolute terms, this is consistent across
     all versions and confirms the value of ensemble diversity.

  2. NTA encoding is the second most important feature (SHAP rank 2). Replacing
     borough-level encoding (5 groups) with NTA-level encoding (212 groups)
     gave the model fine-grained geographic signal. Brooklyn NTAs range from
     $400K median (East New York) to $1.8M median (Brooklyn Heights) — both
     coded as borough=3 without NTA encoding.

  3. Raw mean encoding outperforms Bayesian smoothing for gradient boosted trees
     on this dataset. When bldgclass_encoded is the #1 SHAP feature, shrinking
     it by 20–37% destroys signal. This finding is consistent with literature
     suggesting smoothing is more valuable for neural networks than trees,
     which handle categorical variables more robustly.

  4. 10-fold spatial GroupKFold produces better meta-learner calibration than
     5-fold. The 0.82pp MedAPE improvement from v8 to v9 is attributable
     primarily to this change (verified by ablation: 5000 rounds alone without
     10-fold improved MedAPE by ~0.3pp on the v8 base).

  5. Manhattan is the hardest borough to predict (MedAPE=36.47%). This is not
     a modelling failure — it reflects genuine heterogeneity. Two adjacent units
     in the same building can differ by $1M based on floor number, renovation
     quality, and building prestige, none of which are in the dataset.

10.2 Limitations
-----------------
  1. The model is a snapshot of 2022–2026 market conditions. It cannot
     extrapolate to future market states (e.g., a significant rate drop or
     recession scenario).

  2. The model cannot see interior condition, renovation quality, view, or
     floor number — attributes that dominate luxury segment pricing. This is
     the primary driver of the high Manhattan MedAPE.

  3. The comparable-count flag (SPARSE_MARKET) uses 800m radius but does not
     account for property type — 800 luxury condos within 800m does not
     make the model reliable for a single-family home in that area.

  4. Target encodings are computed on the training set only. A new NTA created
     after the training cutoff (or a building class with zero training examples)
     falls back to the global mean — a safe but uninformative fallback.

  5. The prior_sale_price imputation via assesstot ratios assumes constant
     assessment ratios over time. In rapidly appreciating markets this may
     introduce systematic bias.

10.3 Future Work
-----------------
  1. Floor number and interior features: Partner with a data provider that
     offers listing-level features (StreetEasy, Zillow API) to capture
     floor, renovation year, interior photos (CNN features).

  3. Transformer / tabular deep learning: Recent work (TabNet, FT-Transformer)
     shows competitive performance with gradient boosted trees on large tabular
     datasets. Worth benchmarking on this 185K-row dataset.

  4. Temporal model updating: Retrain quarterly on rolling 3-year window to
     keep the model current as market conditions evolve.

  5. Uncertainty quantification: Replace point predictions with full predictive
     distributions using quantile regression or conformal prediction.

================================================================================
11. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

This paper presented THAMAN, a production-deployed AVM for NYC residential
property valuation. The system achieves R² = 0.6460 and MedAPE = 20.16% on
27,763 unseen holdout sales, outperforming single-model baselines through a
4-model diverse stacking ensemble across 94 features.

The key technical contributions are:
  (a) An end-to-end spatial feature pipeline that converts raw NYC open data
      into 94 model features at inference time in < 5ms using KD-trees.
  (b) Neighbourhood-level (NTA) target encoding that gives the model 212-group
      geographic resolution vs the 5-group borough baseline, augmented by a
      per-NTA OLS price trend slope capturing appreciation momentum.
  (c) Segment-adaptive confidence intervals with a 0–100 score, letter grade,
      and 4 AVM quality flags aligned with 2026 industry standards.
  (d) An NTA inference lookup that resolves lat/lon → ntacode → 4 NTA features
      at prediction time, eliminating the training/serving distribution mismatch
      that would otherwise affect every prediction.

The system is deployed at https://huggingface.co/spaces/Turki-Almurahhem/thaman
and open-sourced at https://github.com/turkialm/thaman-v2.
