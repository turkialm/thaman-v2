═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SECTION 1 — MODEL PERFORMANCE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Q: Why is Riyadh holdout R²=0.79 lower than OOF R²=0.93?

SHORT: OOF uses district-level training data; holdout is 2025 which is new
macro conditions the model hasn't seen.

DETAIL:
- OOF cross-validation splits BY DISTRICT (GroupKFold). Each fold holds out
  entire districts, which avoids spatial autocorrelation leakage.
- However, the training data is district-quarter aggregates — every row
  already encodes the district's median price. When the model sees features
  for district X, those features are strongly correlated with the district's
  own target. This inflates OOF metrics compared to a truly unseen market.
- The holdout period is 2025 Q1–Q3 — entirely new market conditions
  including post-2024 macro shifts (REI index, salary data).
- A gap of ~14 R² points (0.93 → 0.79) is expected and honest. The holdout
  metric is the number to cite: R²=0.79, MedAPE=15.56%.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Your NYC model has R²=0.65 but Riyadh has R²=0.79. Doesn't that make
   NYC worse?

SHORT: Different targets. NYC predicts individual transaction price;
Riyadh predicts district-quarter aggregates. Aggregates are smoother → higher R².

DETAIL:
- NYC: 185K individual transactions. High variance in sale price at parcel
  level (condition, renovation, negotiation). R²=0.65 on individual sales
  is strong — state-of-art NYC AVM models report 0.60–0.70.
- Riyadh: 6,910 district-quarter aggregate rows. Averaging across many
  transactions removes micro-level noise. A model that explains
  district-level medians is an easier task than individual sales.
- Comparing the two R² directly is misleading — apples and oranges.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: MedAPE of 15.56% seems high. Is that good enough?

SHORT: Industry benchmark for AVM in emerging markets is 15–25%.
15.56% is within standard range, especially for district aggregates without
parcel-level data.

DETAIL:
- Zillow Zestimate (US, individual parcels, 30+ years of data): ~7% MedAPE.
- Saudi Arabia has no open parcel-level register. We use Ministry of Justice
  quarterly summaries — district medians, not individual deeds.
- Given the data constraint, 15.56% on 2025 holdout (new macro period) is
  competitive. OOF MedAPE=8.28% shows the model fits well within-sample.
- For context: apartment MedAPE=16.42%, villa=14.46%. Residential plot has
  highest error (25.84%) because land prices are most location-sensitive and
  plot-level features (exact shape, road frontage) are unavailable.

SECTION 2 — DATA
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Q: Why didn't you use individual transaction data for Riyadh?

SHORT: The Ministry of Justice real estate register (Aqarat) is not publicly
accessible. Only quarterly district summaries are released on Saudi Open Data.

DETAIL:
- Saudi MoJ Aqarat database contains individual deed-level data but is
  restricted to licensed valuers and financial institutions.
- Saudi Open Data Platform (data.gov.sa) publishes aggregated quarterly
  reports at district level: transaction count, median price per sqm, volume.
- We used 7 quarterly CSV files (2024 Q1, Q3, Q4; 2025 Q1, Q2, Q3) plus
  a historical XLSX baseline (2018–2023) from the quarterly reports.
- This is not a design choice — it is the data availability constraint.
  If individual deeds were accessible, we would use them.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: How did you get NYC data? Is it reliable?

SHORT: NYC publishes all property sales via ACRIS (open government data).
185K transactions 2022–2026, verified against PLUTO building records.

DETAIL:
- ACRIS (Automated City Register Information System): all NYC property
  transfers, legally recorded. Not scraped — official government database.
- PLUTO (Primary Land Use Tax Lot Output): NYC DCP, covers every tax lot
  with building characteristics (floors, units, built year, FAR, zoning).
- Cross-validated: ACRIS sale price joined to PLUTO on BBL (borough-block-lot).
- Dropped: transfers under $10K (likely intra-family, gift deeds),
  commercial-coded buildings in residential analysis, records with null
  GrossSquareFootage.
- QoL features (311, NYPD complaints, HPD violations, subway distance)
  all from NYC Open Data — same official source.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: How do you validate your Riyadh predictions against real prices?

SHORT: We compared model output to 1,615 active Haraj listings.
Model median was 54% below asking price — consistent with asking vs.
transacted price gap in Saudi market.

DETAIL:
- Scraped 1,615 Haraj.com listings (444 apartments, 630 villas, 526
  residential plots, 15 buildings) with asking price and location.
- THAMAN predicts TRANSACTED price (from MoJ quarterly data).
  Haraj shows ASKING price. In Saudi real estate, asking price is typically
  30–70% above transacted price (negotiation culture, inflated initial ask).
- MedAPE of 54% between THAMAN output and Haraj asking price is expected.
  It is NOT a model error — it is the asking-vs-transacted gap.
- This distinction is important: the model is validated against the same
  data distribution it was trained on (MoJ quarterly medians), not inflated
  asking prices.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Why didn't you scrape more real-time data for Riyadh features?

SHORT: Saudi platforms (Aqar, Haraj) block automated scraping. We used
Saudi Open Data APIs which are official and stable.

DETAIL:
- SA_Aqar rental listings were used for structural medians (bedroom count,
  property age, size) at district level — 1,615 listings, manually curated.
- Open Data APIs used: metro stations, bus stops, commercial services,
  traffic intersections, air quality (NO2, SO2, PM10, O3), all from
  data.gov.sa/smart.gov.sa.
- REI (Real Estate Price Index) from Saudi General Authority for Statistics.
- Salary data from SAMA (Saudi Central Bank) annual statistical report.

SECTION 3 — ALGORITHMS & METHODOLOGY
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Q: Why use a stacking ensemble instead of a single model?

SHORT: Each base learner captures different patterns. Stacking reduces
variance and improves generalization beyond any single model.

DETAIL:
- XGBoost: symmetric trees, good at global feature interactions, fast.
- LightGBM: leaf-wise growth, better at sparse features, handles
  high-cardinality district encoding well.
- CatBoost: ordered boosting, more robust on smaller datasets (Riyadh
  has only 6,910 rows). Dominates Riyadh meta with coefficient 0.721.
- Ridge meta-learner: learns optimal weighted blend of base predictions.
  In NYC: positive=True enforces non-negative weights (prevents cancellation).
  In Riyadh: unconstrained because dataset is smaller and CatBoost dominates.
- OOF (out-of-fold) predictions used to train meta-learner: prevents base
  learners from "cheating" by memorizing training labels.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: What is GroupKFold and why did you use it instead of regular KFold?

SHORT: Regular KFold leaks spatial autocorrelation. Properties in the same
neighborhood have correlated prices — a model can "cheat" by seeing nearby
properties in training and predicting held-out ones.

DETAIL:
- GroupKFold groups entire geographic units (NTA codes for NYC,
  district_ar for Riyadh) into the same fold.
- When fold k is held out, NO property from those neighborhoods appears
  in training. The model must generalize to unseen areas.
- NYC uses 10 folds (311 NTAs → ~31 per fold).
- Riyadh uses 5 folds (147 districts → ~29 per fold).
- Regular KFold on this data would show R²>0.95 (data leakage), giving
  false confidence. GroupKFold gives honest estimates.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: What is SHAP and how does your feature explanation work?

SHORT: SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) decomposes a model's prediction
into individual feature contributions. We approximate it using XGBoost's
built-in feature importance weighted by prediction deviation.

DETAIL:
- True SHAP requires a background dataset and can be slow for real-time API.
- We use XGBoost's `get_booster().predict(pred_contribs=True)` which returns
  per-feature contribution values (TreeSHAP, exact for tree models).
- Top 10 contributors displayed: direction (positive/negative) and magnitude.
- This allows users to understand WHY a property is valued high or low
  (e.g., "Metro proximity pushed price up", "High NO₂ pushed price down").

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Why log-transform the target variable?

SHORT: Property prices are right-skewed (a few luxury properties at 10×
the median distort training). Log-transform makes the target normally
distributed and improves model fit.

DETAIL:
- Raw sale_price has skewness > 5 in NYC, > 3 in Riyadh.
- log1p(price) reduces skewness to near-normal, stabilizing gradient
  descent and preventing the model from fitting outliers disproportionately.
- At inference: prediction = expm1(model_output). MedAPE computed in
  original price space after back-transformation.
- This is standard practice in AVM (automated valuation model) literature.

SECTION 4 — SYSTEM / APPLICATION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Q: How does the app determine which city model to use?

SHORT: Bounding box check on click coordinates. NYC bbox and Riyadh bbox
are non-overlapping — no ambiguity possible.

DETAIL:
- NYC bbox: lat 40.47–40.92, lon -74.26 to -73.70
- Riyadh bbox: lat 24.35–25.10, lon 46.30–47.20
- The two cities are ~8,000 km apart. Clicking outside either bbox shows
  an error. The city mode is set by the UI toggle (NYC/Riyadh button),
  not automatically inferred from coordinates.
- Two completely separate ML pipelines: NYC scorer never sees SAR data,
  Riyadh scorer never sees USD data.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: What happens if a user clicks a location in Riyadh with no nearby
   district data?

SHORT: KDTree nearest-centroid lookup always returns a district. Worst
case is a district with sparse training data → wider confidence interval.

DETAIL:
- 147 district centroids are stored in district_centroids.csv.
- Any coordinate in the Riyadh bbox will match the nearest centroid.
- Districts with fewer than 10 training rows exist (rural outskirts).
  The model uses those districts' features but the confidence interval
  widens because the district MedAPE is not well-estimated.
- Model returns medape_pct from global holdout (15.56%) as fallback
  for confidence interval computation — no district-specific table.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Is this system production-ready?

SHORT: No — it is a research prototype. Limitations include district-level
granularity (not parcel), stale training data, and no legal valuation status.

DETAIL:
- Data freshness: Riyadh model trained on data through 2024, holdout 2025.
  Needs quarterly retraining as new MoJ data becomes available.
- Granularity: Riyadh predicts district medians, not individual property value.
  Individual property features (condition, floor level, view) are not captured.
- Legal: not a licensed valuation. For mortgage/legal purposes, a certified
  valuer (مقيّم معتمد) is required under Saudi Valuers Authority regulations.
- NYC model: individual parcel-level, more granular. Still not licensed AVM.

SECTION 5 — EDGE CASES & KNOWN ISSUES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Q: I noticed the price changed between two identical predictions. Why?

SHORT: Coordinates near a district centroid boundary may match different
districts on repeated calls due to floating-point ordering in KDTree.
Expected variance: ±15% within boundary zones.

DETAIL:
- KDTree uses Euclidean distance on lat/lon. At exact district midpoints,
  the tie-breaking order depends on KDTree internal structure (not guaranteed
  stable across numpy/scipy versions).
- The coordinates 24.7136, 46.6753 sit near the boundary of two districts
  with similar but different median prices (2872 vs 4059 SAR/sqm observed).
- Fix: use a larger k (k=3) and average features. Not implemented in v1
  to keep latency low. Known limitation.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Why does the year default to 2025 even if the user is predicting now (2026)?

SHORT: Model was trained on 2018–2024 data. Using 2026 extrapolates beyond
training range, increasing prediction uncertainty. We cap at 2025 as the
nearest in-distribution year.

DETAIL:
- Riyadh training cutoff: quarter_id < 20251 (data through end of 2024).
- 2025 Q1–Q3 was the holdout set. 2025 is partially observed.
- 2026 is fully out-of-distribution. Extrapolating `sale_year=2026` may
  cause the time-trend features (REI index, salary trend) to produce
  unreliable outputs.
- User can override by supplying year=2024 or year=2025 explicitly.
- NYC model trained on 2022–2026 data → current year is in-distribution.
  No cap applied for NYC.

SECTION 6 — COMPARISON NYC vs RIYADH
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Q: What are the main differences between the NYC and Riyadh models?

NYC MODEL:
- 134 features (vs 76 Riyadh)
- Parcel-level individual transactions (185K rows)
- 10-fold GroupKFold by NTA code
- 4 base learners: XGB-A, XGB-B (structure-only), LGB, CatBoost
- Ridge meta with positive=True (non-negative constraint)
- Target: log1p(sale_price_usd)  → USD total price
- Trained 2022–2026; holdout = newest 15% by date
- R²=0.6509, MedAPE=20.32% on holdout
- Features: structural (sqft, floors, age, units), spatial (subway, parks,
  311 complaints, crime, air quality, NTA income, Airbnb density, FAR)

RIYADH MODEL:
- 140 features (vs 104 NYC)
- District-quarter aggregate rows (6,910 rows)
- 5-fold GroupKFold by district_ar
- 3 base learners: XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost
- Ridge meta unconstrained; CatBoost dominates (coef=0.721)
- Target: log1p(sale_price_sar_sqm) → SAR per sqm
- Trained 2018–2024; holdout = 2025 Q1–Q3
- R²=0.8003, MedAPE=15.56% on holdout
- Features: transit (metro lines, bus BRT), commercial density, air quality
  (NO2/SO2/PM10/O3), mosques, malls, schools, hospitals, parks,
  entertainment, REI price index, SAMA salary data, district aggregates

KEY DIFFERENCES:
1. Data granularity: NYC=parcel, Riyadh=district aggregate
2. NYC has prior sale price imputation (PLUTO assesstot ratios, 99% coverage)
3. Riyadh has Islamic/cultural POIs (mosques) — no NYC equivalent
4. Riyadh has REI macro index (quarterly government price index)
5. NYC uses walk score proxy (weighted POI count); Riyadh uses connectivity score
6. CatBoost dominates Riyadh (small dataset); more balanced in NYC

SECTION 8 — KNOWN LIMITATIONS (say confidently, not defensively)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Riyadh data is district-level aggregate, not individual transactions.
   → "This is the data availability constraint from MoJ. Future work
      could incorporate individual deeds when access is granted."

2. Riyadh model trained through 2024; predictions for 2025–2026 use
   nearest in-distribution year (capped at 2025).
   → "We acknowledge the temporal extrapolation. The holdout on 2025
      data (R²=0.79) validates it remains useful one year forward."

3. No property condition data (renovation quality, interior finish).
   → "Both NYC (PLUTO has limited condition flags) and Riyadh lack this.
      It is a universal limitation of government transaction registers."

4. NYC luxury tier (>$5M) has wider confidence intervals.
   → "Luxury transactions are sparse (< 3% of dataset) so the model
      is less confident there — correctly communicated via a wider range."
